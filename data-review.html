Interpreting the Data
Why European Figures Understate Bird Dropping Impact

The 0.9–5.3% annual soiling loss figures for Europe look reassuringly small — but they are methodologically ill-suited for estimating bird-dropping-specific losses. Six reasons to treat them with caution.

📈

European figures aggregate all soiling types

The 0.9–5.3% annual loss values cover all soiling combined: dust, pollen, industrial particulates, and bird droppings. They are not bird-dropping-specific figures. Bird droppings are only one component of the total, and their individual contribution within that aggregate is not separated out in most European studies.

What these numbers actually measure: total irradiance attenuation from all deposited matter — not bird soiling alone.
🌧

Rain-based models systematically miss bird soiling

Standard European soiling models treat rainfall as the primary cleaning mechanism. Rain effectively removes dust and pollen, but it does not remove bird droppings — uric acid in droppings bonds chemically to glass on drying, forming a cement-like deposit that persists until manually cleaned. Models calibrated with rain-effectiveness assumptions therefore structurally undercount the hard-soiling component.

Bird droppings are classified as hard soiling. Only artificial cleaning removes them. Rain-based models treat them the same as dust — an incorrect assumption.
🐦

European bird populations are not smaller

The key fouling species — rock pigeons, European starlings, herring and black-headed gulls, jackdaws, and rooks — are abundant across Europe, particularly in urban, coastal, and agricultural settings where solar farms are increasingly installed. The UK alone has approximately 100 million breeding birds. There is no biological basis for assuming lower bird-dropping exposure in Europe compared to regions where the controlled studies were conducted.

Rock pigeons: ~28 million pairs in Europe. European starlings: ~60 million pairs. Herring gulls: significant coastal populations across NW Europe.
🌎

Study geographic bias creates a data gap

The vast majority of controlled bird-dropping PV experiments were conducted in India, the UAE, and North Africa — regions with concentrated solar investment and research funding. Peer-reviewed European field data specifically isolating bird-dropping losses is scarce. The apparent "small effect in Europe" largely reflects a lack of measurement rather than a lack of impact. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

Of the 11 primary studies in this review, zero are European field studies isolating bird-dropping losses specifically. All European figures come from aggregated soiling models.
🔌

Soiling sensors don't capture bypass-diode losses

Standard soiling sensors and IV-curve tracers are designed to measure gradual, spatially uniform irradiance attenuation — the dust model. Bird droppings create non-uniform hard shading that triggers bypass diodes, producing stepped, disproportionate power losses. This mechanism is largely invisible to standard monitoring equipment, so it tends to be misclassified as "unexplained performance loss" rather than attributed to bird soiling.

A single dropping covering one cell can trigger a bypass diode and remove ~33% of module output — this shows up as an electrical anomaly, not a soiling event, in standard monitoring.
📍

National averages mask extreme site-level variation

Soiling from bird droppings is highly site-specific, driven by proximity to roost sites, water bodies, power lines, and urban areas. A solar farm near a gull colony or a city-centre pigeon population may experience 5–15× the annual loss of a rural site with sparse bird activity. National or regional averages — even when accurate for their scope — are poor predictors of individual site performance and can create dangerous false confidence for site operators in high-exposure locations.

Urban/coastal sites with heavy pigeon or gull activity: up to 35% instantaneous power loss documented in field surveys. Rural sites: often <2%.
Degradation Mechanisms
How Bird Droppings Reduce PV Output

Two simultaneous mechanisms act in parallel when bird droppings land on a panel.

■

Hard Shading & Bypass Diode Triggering

Bird droppings create opaque "hard shading" — fundamentally different from soft soiling (dust). A single dropping covering one cell in a string limits the current of the entire string. Bypass diodes activate, shedding the shaded substring.

33% module power can be lost from shading a single cell (1 of 3 bypass diode substrings). Source: multiple studies.
▲

Hot-Spot Formation

Shaded cells are forced to act as resistors, dissipating electrical energy as heat. Dropping-induced hot spots can reach very high temperatures, accelerating module degradation and posing a long-term reliability risk.

Panel back-surface: 59–60 °C (soiled) vs 29–33 °C (clean). Each +1 °C reduces output by 0.2–0.5%. Source: Case Studies Thermal Eng., 2023.
●

Surface Adhesion (Sticky Soiling)

Uric acid in droppings bonds to the glass surface when dry, forming a cement-like residue. Unlike dust, rain does not remove it. Droppings can spread to 5–6 inches across the surface and accumulate into clusters.

Bird droppings account for 46–89% of total measured efficiency loss in studies where multiple soiling types coexist. Source: Scientific Reports, 2024.
♯

Thermal Compounding Loss

Hot-spot-induced temperature rise adds a secondary power loss on top of the primary shading loss. A panel running 25 °C hotter than its clean counterpart loses an additional 5–12.5% of output from the temperature coefficient penalty alone.

Combined shading + thermal loss observed: up to 23.8% in controlled dual-mechanism study. Source: Scientific Reports, 2024.
Accumulation Rates
Daily Soiling Rate Context

General soiling accumulation rates from field measurements. Bird droppings contribute episodically (not continuously) but at a disproportionately high per-event impact. Once present, they remain until manually removed.
